                <h1 class="post-title">What is Augmented Reality (AR) and How does it work</h1>
            </center>
            <div class="post-content postContent lazyLoad" data-id="19762">
                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Wondering what a superimposition and haptic communication are? What are smart glasses and head-mounted displays? What is Augmented Reality and Virtual Reality? What's mobile computing? What do all these digital trends mean? Are you ever going to catch up?... Don't worry, breathe, keep calm and we are here to help you sort things out.</span></p>
                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">So you've heard something about AR/VR/MR and would like to know more. For most people, it is still a quite abstract and exotic technology, often perceived as the science-fiction out of Hollywood movies. Animate holograms, interactive displays, and virtual 3D models. In fact, all these things do already exist.</span></p>
                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Real environment modified by computer-generated objects is present in many areas, from aviation to gaming, we're just not aware of it as users. Have you tried to catch Pokemon in recent years or to fit furniture in your room via IKEA app? That's AR, and it has more far-reaching areas of potential use. It is still in <a target="_blank" href="https://thinkmobiles.com/blog/augmented-reality-companies/">development</a> and multiple engineers and tech companies around the world are working to enhance it. In the meantime, let's find out what is Augmented Reality, and let's start with this vision by Magic Leap. Exciting!</span></p>

                <center>
                    <h2>What is Augmented Reality?</h2>
                </center>
                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Augmented reality is the technology that expands our physical world, adding layers of digital information onto it. Unlike Virtual Reality (VR), AR does not create the whole artificial environments to replace real with a virtual one. AR appears in direct view of an existing environment and adds&nbsp;</span>
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;">sounds, videos, graphics to it.</span>
                </p>

                <div class="text-highlight" id="ar">
                    <p class="text-highlight-info">A view of the physical real-world environment with superimposed computer-generated images, thus changing the perception of reality, is the AR.</p>
                </div>


                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The term itself was coined back in 1990, and one of the first commercial uses were in television and military. With the rise of the Internet and smartphones, AR rolled out its second wave and now</span>
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;">adays is mostly related to the interactive concept. 3D models are directly projected onto physical things or fused together in real-time, various <a target="_blank" href="https://thinkmobiles.com/blog/augmented-reality-retail/" rel="noopener">augmented reality apps</a>                    impact our habits, social life, and the entertainment industry.</span>
                </p>
                <p>AR apps typically connect digital animation to a special 'marker', or with the help of GPS in phones pinpoint the location. Augmentation is happening in real time and within the context of the environment, for example, overlaying scores
                    to a live feed sport events.</p>
                <p>There are 4 types of augmented reality today:</p>
                <ul>
                    <li>markerless AR</li>
                    <li>marker-based AR</li>
                    <li>projection-based AR</li>
                    <li>superimposition-based AR</li>
                </ul>
                <p></p>
                <center>
                    <h2>Brief history of AR</h2>
                </center>
                <p><b>AR in the 1960s.&nbsp;</b>In 1968 Ivan Sutherland&nbsp;and&nbsp;Bob Sproull created a first head-mounted display, they called it&nbsp;<a target="_blank" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NtwZXGprxag" rel="noopener noreferrer">The Sword of Damocles</a>
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;">. Obviously, it was a rough device that displayed primitive computer graphics.</span>
                </p>
                <p><b>AR in the 1970s.&nbsp;</b>In 1975 Myron Krueger<span style="font-weight: 400;"> created </span><a target="_blank" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dmmxVA5xhuo" rel="noopener noreferrer">Videoplace</a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> - an artificial reality laboratory. The scientist envisioned the interaction with digital stuff by human movements. This concept later was used for certain projectors, video cameras, and onscreen silhouettes.&nbsp;</span></p>
                <p><b>AR in the 1980s.&nbsp;</b>In 1980 Steve Mann<span style="font-weight: 400;"> developed a first portable computer called </span><a target="_blank" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jSAGHqcVupE" rel="noopener noreferrer">EyeTap</a>
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;">, designed to be worn in front of the eye. It recorded the scene to superimposed effects on it later, and show it all to a user who could also play with it via head movements.&nbsp;</span>In 1987 Douglas
                    George and Robert Morris<span style="font-weight: 400;"> developed the prototype of a&nbsp;</span>heads-up display (HUD)<span style="font-weight: 400;">. It displayed astronomical data over the real sky.&nbsp;</span></p>
                <p><b>AR in the 1990s. </b>The year&nbsp;1990<span style="font-weight: 400;"> marked the birth of the </span>"augmented reality" term<span style="font-weight: 400;">. It first appeared in the work of Thomas&nbsp;</span>Caudell and David Mizell
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;">&nbsp;- Boeing company researchers.&nbsp;</span>In 1992 Louis Rosenberg<span style="font-weight: 400;">&nbsp;of the US Air Force created the AR system called </span>"Virtual Fixtures"<span style="font-weight: 400;">.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span>In
                    1999, a<span style="font-weight: 400;"> group of scientists led by </span>Frank Delgado and Mike Abernathy<span style="font-weight: 400;"> tested new navigation software, which generated&nbsp;</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">runways and streets data from a helicopter video.</span></p>
                <p><b>AR in the 2000s.&nbsp;</b>In 2000 a<span style="font-weight: 400;"> Japanese scientist </span>Hirokazu Kato<span style="font-weight: 400;"> developed and published </span><a target="_blank" href="https://artoolkit.org/" rel="noopener noreferrer">ARToolKit</a>&nbsp;
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;"> - an open-source SDK. Later it was adjusted&nbsp;</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">to work with Adobe</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">.&nbsp;</span>In 2004 Trimble Navigation<span style="font-weight: 400;"> presented an outdoor helmet-mounted AR system.&nbsp;</span>In
                    2008 Wikitude<span style="font-weight: 400;"> made the&nbsp;</span><a target="_blank" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8EA8xlicmT8" rel="noopener noreferrer">AR Travel Guide</a>&nbsp;<span style="font-weight: 400;">for Android mobile devices.&nbsp;</span></p>
                <p><b>AR today.&nbsp;</b>In 2013 Google<span style="font-weight: 400;"> beta tested the&nbsp;</span>Google Glass - with<span style="font-weight: 400;">&nbsp;internet connection via Bluetooth.&nbsp;</span>In 2015 Microsoft presented two&nbsp;
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;">brand new technologies: </span>Windows Holographic<span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span>HoloLens (an AR goggles with lots of se<span style="font-weight: 400;">nsors to display HD holograms).&nbsp;</span>In
                    2016 Niantic<span style="font-weight: 400;"> launched </span>
                    <a target="_blank" href="http://www.pokemongo.com/" rel="noopener noreferrer">Pokemon Go</a><span style="font-weight: 400;">&nbsp;game for mobile devices. The app blew the gaming industry up and earned $2 million in a just first week.&nbsp;</span></p>
                <center>
                    <h2>How does Augmented Reality work</h2>
                </center>
                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;"> What is Augmented Reality for many of us implies a technical side, i.e. how does AR&nbsp;work? For AR a certain range of data (images, animations, videos, 3D models) may be used and people will see the result in both natural and synthetic light. Also, users are aware of being in the real world which is advanced by computer vision, unlike in VR.</span></p>
                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">AR can be displayed on various devices: screens, glasses, handheld devices, mobile phones, head-mounted displays. It involves technologies like <strong>S.L.A.M.</strong> (s</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">imultaneous localization and mapping), <strong>d</strong></span><b>epth tracking&nbsp;</b>(briefly,
                    a s<span style="font-weight: 400;">ensor data calculating the distance to the objects), and the following <strong>components</strong>:</span></p>
                <ul>
                    <li><strong>Cameras and sensors.</strong> Collecting data about user's interactions and sending it for processing. Cameras on devices are scanning the surroundings and with this info, a device locates physical objects and generates 3D
                        models. It may be special duty cameras, like in Microsoft Hololens, or common smartphone cameras to take pictures/videos.</li>
                    <li><strong>Processing.&nbsp;</strong>AR devices eventually should act like little computers, something modern smartphones already do. In the same manner, they require a CPU, a GPU, flash memory, RAM, Bluetooth/WiFi, a GPS, etc. to be
                        able to measure speed, angle, direction, orientation in space, and so on.</li>
                    <li><strong>Projection.</strong> This refers to&nbsp;a miniature projector on AR headsets, which takes data from sensors and projects digital content (result of processing) onto a surface to view. In fact, the use of projections in AR
                        has not been fully invented yet to use it in commercial products or services.</li>
                    <li><strong>Reflection.</strong>&nbsp;Some AR devices have mirrors to assist human eyes to view virtual images. Some have an "array of small curved mirrors" and some have a double-sided mirror to reflect light to a camera and to a user's
                        eye. The goal of such reflection paths is to perform a proper image alignment.</li>
                </ul>

                    <h2>Augmented reality devices</h2>
                </center>
                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Many modern devices already support Augmented reality. From smartphones and tablets to gadgets like </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Google Glass</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">&nbsp;or</span>
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;">&nbsp;handheld devices, and these&nbsp;</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">technologies continue to evolve. For processing and projection, AR devices and hardware, first of all, have r</span>
                    <span style="font-weight: 400;">equirements such as <strong>s</strong></span><b>ensors, cameras, </b><strong>accelerometer, gyroscope, digital compass, GPS, CPU, displays,</strong>and things we've already mentioned.</p>
                <p>Devices suitable for Augmented reality fall into the following categories:</p>
                <ul>
                    <li><span style="font-weight: 400;"><strong>Mobile devices</strong> (smartphones and tablets) - the most available and best fit for AR mobile</span>&nbsp;apps, ranging from pure gaming and entertainment to business analytics, sports, and
                        social networking.
                    </li>
                    <li><strong>Special AR devices</strong>, de<span style="font-weight: 400;">signed primarily and solely for augmented reality experiences. One example is h</span>ead-up displays (HUD), sending data to a transparent display directly into
                        user's view. Originally introduced to train military fighters pilots, now such devices have applications in aviation, automotive industry, manufacturing, sports, etc.</li>
                    <li><b>AR glasses (or smart glasses) </b>– Google Glasses, Meta 2 Glasses, Laster See-Thru, Laforge AR eyewear, etc. These units are capable of displaying notifications from your smartphone, assisting assembly line workers, access content
                        hands-free, etc.</li>
                    <li><b>AR contact lenses (or smart lenses),&nbsp;</b>taking Augmented Reality one step even farther. Manufacturers like Samsung and Sony have announced the development of AR lenses. Respectively,&nbsp;<span style="font-weight: 400;">Samsung is working on lenses as the accessory to smartphones, while&nbsp;</span>
                        <span style="font-weight: 400;">Sony is designing lenses as separate AR devices (with features like taking photos or storing data).&nbsp;</span>
                    </li>
                    <li><b>Virtual retinal displays (</b><strong>VRD)</strong>,&nbsp; creating images by projecting laser light into the human eye. Aiming at bright, high contrast and high-resolution images, such systems yet remain to be made for a practical
                        use.
                    </li>
                </ul>

                    <h2>Possible applications of AR</h2>
                </center>
                <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Augmented reality may complement our everyday activities in various ways. For instance, one of the most popular applications of&nbsp;</span>AR is<b> gaming.&nbsp;</b>New AR games&nbsp;<span style="font-weight: 400;">provide much better experiences to players, some even promote a more active outgoing way of life (PokemonGo, Ingress). Gaming&nbsp;</span>grounds
                    are being moved from virtual spheres to real life, and players actually perform certain activities. For instance, a simple gym activity for kids by the&nbsp;<span style="font-weight: 400;">Canadian company SAGA, where to crack cubes moving on a wall children hit it with a ball. </span></p>
                <p></p>
                <p><b>AR in retail</b>&nbsp;may act to bring better customer engagement and retention, as well as br<span style="font-weight: 400;">and awareness and more sales. Some features may also help customers make wiser purchases – providing product data with 3D models of any size or color. <strong>R</strong></span><b>eal-estate&nbsp;</b>can
                    also benefit from Augmented Reality via&nbsp;<span style="font-weight: 400;">3D tours of apartments and houses, that can also be manipulated to amend some parts.</span></p>
                <p>Other potential areas for AR include:</p>
                <ul>
                    <li>Education: interactive models for learning and training purposes, from mathematics to chemistry.</li>
                    <li>Medicine/healthcare: to help diagnose, monitor, train, localize, etc.</li>
                    <li>Military: for advanced navigation, marking objects in real time.</li>
                    <li>Art / installations / visual arts / music.</li>
                    <li>Tourism: data on destinations, sightseeing objects, navigation, and directions.</li>
                    <li>Broadcasting: enhancing live events and event streaming by overlaying content.</li>
                    <li>Industrial design: to visualize, calculate or model.</li>
                </ul>
